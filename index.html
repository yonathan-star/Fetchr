Background & Market Research

Research Paper

The Problem

Dog waste left on sidewalks and public walkways is one of the most persistent urban hygiene issues in the United States. More than 40 million households own at least one dog, and on average, a single dog produces around 275 pounds of waste per year. Despite widespread awareness of the sanitation and health risks, a significant portion of this waste is never collected.

Studies by the EPA have identified dog waste as a top-three contributor to bacteria in urban stormwater runoff. Waste left on sidewalks contains E. coli, Salmonella, Giardia, and roundworm eggs that can survive in soil for weeks and wash into drainage systems during rain events. The public health impact is measurable and well-documented.

Yet the behavior change required to solve this problem is simple and obvious: pick it up. The barrier is not knowledge or intent. The barrier is friction. Carrying bags, bending down, tying the bag, carrying it to a trash can — all while managing a dog on a leash — is enough of an inconvenience that many owners skip it.

Existing Solutions and Their Limits

Current solutions fall into two categories: convenience improvements for manual collection (biodegradable bags, scooper tools, leash-attached dispensers) and yard-specific cleaning services that operate when the owner is not present. Neither category addresses the core problem during an active walk.

No product on the consumer market autonomously handles waste collection in real time during a dog walk. This is the gap Fetchr fills.

The Technology Window

Several enabling technologies have matured enough to make an autonomous walk-following waste collection robot feasible at the prototype level. YOLOv5 object detection runs efficiently on embedded microcontrollers. Servo motors are inexpensive and precise. Bluetooth Low Energy provides reliable proximity tracking. 3D printing in PETG allows rapid custom fabrication. The iRobot Create 2 Pro provides a reliable programmable base platform. Taken together, these tools create a realistic path from concept to working prototype at a student project budget.

Define

Purpose for Innovation

Dog waste on public sidewalks is a sanitation and public health problem that affects every urban and suburban community with a significant dog-owning population. The problem is not one of awareness — most dog owners know they should pick up after their pets. The problem is one of friction: the act of bending, bagging, tying, and carrying waste is inconvenient enough that many owners skip it, especially during longer walks or in moments of distraction.

Existing technology has not addressed this specific moment. Yard-cleaning robots, smart feeders, collar trackers — none of them solve the problem that happens on the sidewalk, mid-walk, when the owner's hands are full and the dog is already pulling on the leash.

Fetchr exists to remove that friction entirely. The owner does not change their behavior. They walk their dog. The robot handles the rest.

The innovation is not the hardware. The innovation is the idea that this moment in a dog walk does not have to involve the owner at all.

Define

Thesis Statement

Dog waste left on public walkways poses health and sanitation risks that current solutions fail to address automatically. Fetchr is a walk-following robot that detects, scoops, and stores dog waste in real time using AI vision and a robotic arm, removing the need for owner intervention during walks.

Abstract

Dog waste on sidewalks and public walkways is a widespread sanitation problem. Existing solutions require manual effort from pet owners, and no autonomous product currently exists that handles waste collection during the walk itself. Fetchr addresses this gap by combining a walk-following robot base with an onboard AI detection system and a mechanical scooping arm.

The robot uses Bluetooth RSSI to follow its owner during dog walks. When the dog squats, a YOLOv5 model running on an ESP32 detects the posture change, and a second model confirms waste on the ground. A dual-servo arm then extends, scoops the waste into a sealed onboard bin, and retracts. After the walk, the robot returns to a smart charging dock equipped with a color sensor, moisture probe, and load cell that analyze the collected waste for health indicators and send the data to a companion app via WiFi.

The prototype is built on an iRobot Create 2 Pro base with custom 3D-printed PETG components designed in Fusion 360. Fetchr removes a daily friction point for pet owners while improving public hygiene and offering a passive window into pet health.

Ideate — Brainstorm

Approach Reasoning

Why a Walk-Following Robot, Not a Yard Robot

The most common misconception about this type of product is that it should be a yard cleaner — a robot that patrols a yard on a schedule and picks up what it finds. This was the first concept considered and the first one rejected.

The problem is not in the yard. Most dog owners have a yard that they can manage. The problem is on public sidewalks during walks, where the dog deposits waste that the owner then has to carry. A yard robot does not solve this problem at all.

Fetchr solves the problem where the problem happens: mid-walk, on the sidewalk, in real time.

Why Dual-Model Detection

A single model trained to detect waste on the ground would have too high a false positive rate. Dogs sniff the ground, leaves fall, shadows appear. A single model cannot reliably distinguish all of these from an actual waste event.

The dual-model approach uses squat posture detection as a gating signal. A dog squatting and waste appearing on the ground at the same time and place is a much stronger signal than either alone. This design decision was validated by survey data showing reliability as the #2 barrier to purchase (56% of respondents).

Why RSSI Following Instead of GPS

GPS is accurate to 2–5 meters under ideal conditions. A dog walk requires maintaining a 1–2 meter follow distance. GPS precision is insufficient for this use case. Bluetooth RSSI signal strength, combined with simple proportional steering control, provides sufficient precision at pedestrian walking speeds.

Engineering Drawings

Pseudocode — Main Control Loop

// ── FETCHR MAIN CONTROL LOOP (ESP32 Rover) ──

INITIALIZE:
  connect to iRobot Create 2 via serial (OI mode)
  start BLE scan for owner phone beacon
  load YOLOv5 Model 1 (squat detection)
  load YOLOv5 Model 2 (waste detection)
  set state = FOLLOWING
  set arm = HOME

LOOP:
  IF state == FOLLOWING:
    rssi = read_ble_signal_strength()
    drive_command = compute_follow_direction(rssi)
    send_to_create2(drive_command)

    frame = capture_camera_frame()
    squat_score = run_model_1(frame)

    IF squat_score > THRESHOLD_1:
      waste_score = run_model_2(frame)
      IF waste_score > THRESHOLD_2
         AND persistent_for(3 frames):
        set state = COLLECTING
        set state timer = now()

  IF state == COLLECTING:
    send_to_create2(STOP)
    deploy_arm_sequence():
      servo1.move_to(EXTEND_ANGLE)
      wait(200ms)
      servo2.move_to(OPEN_ANGLE)
      wait(300ms)
      servo1.sweep_forward()
      servo2.close_cup()
      servo1.retract_to_bin()
      servo2.release_over_bin()
      servo1.move_to(HOME)
    seal_bin()
    set state = FOLLOWING
    log_collection_event(timestamp)

  IF battery_low() OR bin_full():
    set state = DOCKING
    navigate_to_dock_beacon()

  IF state == DOCKING AND at_dock():
    set state = CHARGING
    trigger_dock_analysis()

END LOOP

Test & Reflect

Iterative Reflections

01
FAILUREServo stall during arm deployment on uneven grass

Root cause: Rover not perfectly level on grass. Arm encountered unexpected resistance mid-sweep due to unexpected load angle.

Fix: Slowed deploy PWM, added 200ms pause after extension before sweep begins. Allows servo to settle before load increases.

02
FAILUREModel 2 false positive on dark leaf in grass

Root cause: Wet dark leaf triggered waste detection above confidence threshold in daylight outdoor test.

Fix: Raised Model 2 confidence threshold from 0.65 to 0.75. Added 3-frame persistence requirement before issuing trigger.

03
FAILUREFusion 360 MCP body name drift during CAD operations

Root cause: combine_by_name with new_body_name caused Fusion to append _2, _3 suffixes after each operation, breaking all subsequent references.

Fix: Stopped using new_body_name in combine ops. Implemented dummy 1×1×1mm box join trick to rename bodies. Verify state with get_bounding_box before each batch.

04
FAILURERSSI tracking lost at fast walking pace

Root cause: BLE scan rate (1 Hz) too slow at brisk pace; rover steered to last known direction instead of updating.

Fix: Increased BLE scan to 4 Hz. Raised motor speed ceiling so rover can close distance faster. Note: normal dog walk pace is 2–3 km/h, well within range.

05
FAILUREPartial scoop capture on soft-consistency samples

Root cause: Original scoop cup profile could not fully contain loose samples during upswing retraction.

Fix: Redesigned cup with tighter rim closure angle and deeper cup body. Slowed retraction servo speed by 15%. Full resolution for all consistency types targeted in next hardware revision.

Test & Reflect

Possible Improvements

Infrared Illumination for Low-Light Detection

Model 1 struggled in low-light conditions (Trial 7). Adding an IR LED ring around the camera would improve detection at dusk or night without visible light disturbance to the dog.

Redesigned Scoop Cup for Soft Consistency

Partial captures on soft samples are the primary scooping weakness. A version 2 cup with a wider mouth and tighter rim closure is the next mechanical improvement targeted.

Custom PCB to Replace Dev Boards

The current prototype uses two separate ESP32 dev boards. A custom PCB integrating both into a single board with onboard voltage regulation would reduce size and improve reliability.

Bin Self-Emptying at Dock

Survey respondents listed "easy to empty" as the #1 desired feature. A fully automated bin dump sequence at the dock — where waste transfers directly into an outdoor trash liner — would remove the last manual step.

Speed-Adaptive Following

The current RSSI algorithm uses a fixed speed ceiling. A PID controller that smoothly adjusts speed based on signal drop rate would handle varied owner pacing more gracefully.

Broader Dog Breed Training Dataset

Model 1 was trained on a limited breed set. Expanding the squat posture training dataset to cover more body sizes and coat patterns would improve generalizability.

The robot uses Bluetooth RSSI to follow its owner during dog walks. A dual-servo arm then extends, scoops the waste into a sealed onboard bin, and retracts. After the walk, the robot returns to a smart charging dock equipped with a color sensor, moisture probe, and load cell that analyze the collected waste for health indicators and send the data to a companion app via WiFi.

The prototype is built on an iRobot Create 2 Pro base with custom 3D-printed PETG components designed in Fusion 360. Fetchr removes a daily friction point for pet owners while improving public hygiene and offering a passive window into pet health.

Prototype

How It Works

Step 1
Owner begins the walk

Rover powers on. ESP32 acquires the owner's phone via BLE RSSI. Rover begins following at 1–2 meter distance behind owner and dog.

Step 2
Model 1 — Squat posture detected

Camera streams continuously. YOLOv5 Model 1 monitors the dog's body. When a squat posture is detected above confidence threshold, Model 2 is triggered.

Step 3
Model 2 — Waste confirmed on ground

Model 2 scans the ground region. If waste is confirmed above threshold for 3 consecutive frames, a collection trigger is issued.

Step 4
Arm deploys and scoops

Rover stops. Dual-servo arm extends, scoops waste into sealed PETG bin, retracts. Total collection window: under 15 seconds.

Step 5
Walk resumes automatically

Arm retracted. Bin sealed. ESP32 resumes RSSI tracking. Rover re-joins the walk. Owner never stopped.

Step 6
Return to dock

When battery drops or bin fills, rover navigates to iRobot home base beacon and docks autonomously.

Step 7
Health analysis + app report

Dock sensors (color, moisture, weight) analyze collected sample. ESP32 pushes health summary to companion app via WiFi.

Business Pitch

The Problem

40 million US households own dogs. Every single one of those owners walks their dog. And every one of them has to stop, bend down, bag waste, tie it, and carry it. It is the worst two minutes of every dog owner's day. Nobody has solved it yet.

The Solution

Fetchr is a walk-following robot that detects, scoops, and stores dog waste automatically — so the owner never has to stop. The robot follows you. When your dog goes, Fetchr handles it. You keep walking.

How It Makes Money

  • Hardware: $299 per unit (rover + dock)
  • Consumables: $8–15/month in bin liners and cup inserts
  • Fetchr Health+: $7.99/month subscription for extended health data, vet reports, and alerts

$261 billion pet care industry. 40 million dog-owning households. Zero existing solutions in this specific category. 91% of our 101 survey respondents had never heard of a walk-following waste robot. The white space is real.

We are seeking mentorship and resources to take Fetchr from a working prototype to a manufacturable product. The technology works. The market wants it. The next step is refinement, manufacturing readiness, and a crowdfunding launch.

Presentation

Elevator Pitch

Every dog owner knows the worst part of the walk. The dog goes. You stop. You pull out a bag. You bend down. You tie it. You carry it to the next trash can. It is the most unpleasant two minutes of an otherwise enjoyable routine — and nobody has automated it yet.

Fetchr is a robot that follows you during your dog walk. When your dog defecates, two AI models running onboard detect the event — one watching your dog's posture, one watching the ground. When both agree, the robot's arm extends, scoops the waste into a sealed bin, and retracts. The walk continues. You never stopped.

After the walk, the robot returns to its dock, which analyzes the collected waste and sends health data to your phone. So every walk also becomes a passive health check for your dog.

The market is 40 million dog-owning households. The product does not exist yet. We are building it.
